Machine Learning–Based Digital Addiction Risk Model

An analytical system designed to estimate digital addiction risk levels using behavioral survey data and predictive modeling techniques.

Problem Statement

The municipality lacks a standardized analytical mechanism to quantify digital addiction risk among students and young individuals. Existing assessments are largely descriptive and do not provide predictive insights. This limits the ability to identify risk patterns, compare populations, or support evidence-based planning for digital well-being initiatives.

Model Objectives

Develop a supervised machine learning model to predict addiction risk
Quantify relationships between digital usage behaviors and risk levels
Classify individuals into Low, Moderate, or High risk categories
Evaluate model accuracy, robustness, and generalizability
Generate analytical outputs suitable for research reporting and policy planning (non-clinical use)

Model Scope

  • Definition of behavioral features related to digital usage
  • Use of anonymized, survey-based input data
  • Data preprocessing, encoding, and normalization
  • Exploratory data analysis and feature selection
  • Training and testing multiple ML algorithms
  • Performance evaluation using standard metrics
  • Interpretation of feature influence on predictions
  • Technical documentation of the final model

Out of Scope

  • Clinical diagnosis or mental health treatment
  • Real-time monitoring or continuous data collection
  • Public dashboards or decision-automation systems
  • Mobile or cloud application deployment
  • Integration with external databases or APIs
  • Operational use beyond analytical planning

Assumptions & Constraints

  • Self-reported data reasonably reflects user behavior
  • Sufficient sample size for model training
  • Ethical approval and informed consent obtained
  • Strict privacy and anonymization practices
  • Model intended for analytical use only

Model Risks

  • Bias due to self-reported survey responses
  • Class imbalance affecting prediction accuracy
  • Overfitting or limited generalization
  • Misinterpretation of outputs without context
  • Use beyond intended analytical scope
